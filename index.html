How this data was gathered and derived

Every measurement in this dashboard comes from public water quality monitoring records. C-HAWQ did not collect these samples; we retrieved, cleaned, and summarized data that monitoring agencies have already published, so the map and tables reflect what those agencies reported.

Where the data comes from

All measurements are drawn from the Water Quality Portal, a public database operated jointly by the U.S. Geological Survey (USGS), the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), and the National Water Quality Monitoring Council. The Portal gathers monitoring results submitted by federal, state, and regional programs, including the Florida Department of Environmental Protection and the state's five water management districts. Each record is one measurement taken at one monitoring station on one date, paired with the station's latitude and longitude as reported to the Portal.

Scope and cleaning

The records cover monitoring stations across Florida; the exact date range for each parameter appears on its card, generally running from 2010 to the present. Before anything is displayed, records missing a value, a date, or a station are dropped, and values outside physically possible ranges are removed to exclude obvious data-entry errors (for example, dissolved oxygen above 30 mg/L or pH outside 0 to 14). The build date for the current data set is shown just above this section.

How the numbers are calculated

  • Data points, stations, and time frame come straight from the cleaned records for each parameter.
  • Sampling cadence is the median number of days between consecutive samples at a typical station.
  • Monthly mean is the average of a station's measurements within a calendar month.
  • Yearly observations and mean (in each station's pop-up) are the count and average for a calendar year, expandable to a month-by-month breakdown for seasonal comparison.
  • City average pools every station inside a searched boundary and averages their measurements by month.

The map

Station locations use the coordinates reported to the Water Quality Portal. The base map and place boundaries come from OpenStreetMap, and place searches are resolved with OpenStreetMap's Nominatim geocoder. A station counts as inside a city when its coordinates fall within that city's boundary.

What to keep in mind

These values are presented as reported by the contributing agencies. Sampling is uneven across stations and years, so a station with few records carries less weight than one sampled often; the minimum-records filter helps account for this. All averages are simple arithmetic means, and the city average combines stations that can behave differently, so read it as a general signal for an area rather than a precise figure.
